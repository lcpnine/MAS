\begin{abstract}
\begin{quote}
In this report we present a six agent system designed for the TileWorld simulation using the MASON framework. In this multi agent benchmarking simulation agents need to work together to explore a partially observable grid, fetch and deliver limited-lifetime tiles while also discovering the initially hidden fuel station. Our approach uses a two-layer architecture with a shared basis including A* pathfinding, fuel-aware planning, memory, and communication protocol; and a specialization layer with a specific role assigned to each agent. Throughout our development phase, our average reward in Config~1 improved from 32.1 to 864.3 and an average reward of 888.6 in Config~2 (for validation) with zero failures, despite the elevated challenges in Config~2. The largest recorded gain came from introducing multi-agent coordination in Phase~3 of our development, while Phase~4 further improved our scores with the introduction of role-specific behavior grounded in the bold-vs-cautious agent framework of Pollack and Ringuette (1990).
\end{quote}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The Tile World simulation a famous testing environment for agent architectures under resource 
constraints with partial observability \cite{pollack1990}. In this environmnet six agents are 
placed in a bounded grid to cooperate and earn rewards from picking up tiles and delivering them 
to holes within a fixed lenght experiment. 

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.75\columnwidth]{TileWorld.png}
  \caption{The TileWorld simulation environment.}
  \label{fig:tileworld}
\end{figure}

This environment is particullarly challanging because the agents can only see a  predifined local
 area around them while both tiles and holes expiring after a limited number of steps, making timing 
 and up-to-date global information critical. Agent are also subject to a constraint of fuel usage, 
 as every step drains their tank, that can only be refilled at a single (initially hidden) fuelstation. 

Based on these constraints it is easy to see how a naive approach - with six independent agents each 
running the same generic loop - is sub-optimal as it wastes effort through redundant exploration, duplicate 
targeting, and identical routing. Thus, our goal was to engineer a complex agent architecture, communication
 and planning protocol to make the six agent behave like a coordinated team while keeping each agent's 
 design tractable and individually testable.

Our solution consists of a \emph{two-layer architecture}. The base layer is shared among all the agents 
with three implemented classes \texttt{SmartTWAgent} (the base agent), \texttt{SmartTWAgentMemory} 
(time-stamped shared memory), and \texttt{SmartTWPlanner} (goal-directed, fuel-aware planning). 
This is then complemented with a role specific layer, to make six specialised agents, with different 
priorities: fuel discovery, tile collection, delivery reliability, map coverage, high-value routing, 
and failure prevention.


% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Background}

The TileWorld simulation was introduced by Pollack and Ringuette \shortcite{pollack1990} 
to provide an experimental to study agent deliberation. Their main objective was to see 
what the trade-off is between \emph{bold} agents that commit to plans and execute them 
sequentially without interruption, and \emph{cautious} agents that iteratively reconsider 
their plan during execution. In their findings they concluded that neither extreme dominates 
the other, and the optimal balance largely dependent on the rate of change in the environment. 

Subsequent work surveyed in \cite{kinny1993} confirmed that in high-volatility settings 
(many objects and/or short lifetimes) cautious behaviour is advantageuous while in lower-volatility 
environments (sparse, long-lived objects) the committed agent will yield to higher rewards.

In our development we considered these inshights and within the shared layer we defined 
parameters that depend on the volatility of the environment, that is detected on-the-fly. 
We also introduced the \texttt{SmarterReplanningAgent} with specialist functions as a configurable 
cautious agent: in higher-volatility conditions it calculates whether a target will survive until 
arrival and decides to pursue that given target accordingly. In stable conditions it uses a looser
 threshold and commits longer.

\section{System Architecture}

\subsection{Two-Layer Design}

The architecture separates concerns into two layers.

\textbf{Layer~1 --- Shared Foundation.}
All six agents inherit from \texttt{SmartTWAgent} and use the same
\texttt{SmartTWAgentMemory} and \texttt{SmartTWPlanner}. This layer provides:
A* pathfinding with obstacle avoidance and fuel awareness;
a memory store with per-cell observation timestamps and automatic staleness
tracking;
a claim system to prevent two agents targeting the same object;
runtime environment classification (\texttt{isDense()},
\texttt{isShortLifetime()}, \texttt{isLargeGrid()});
and a full broadcast communication stack.

\textbf{Layer~2 --- Specialist Agents.}
Each specialist extends \texttt{SmartTWAgent} and overrides the \texttt{think()}
method to insert role-specific decisions before delegating to the shared base
logic via \texttt{super.think()}. This keeps each specialist focused on its role
while retaining all base capabilities unchanged.

\subsection{Shared Agent Loop}

Every simulation step follows a fixed ordering enforced by the MASON scheduler:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Environment step} (ordering 1): new objects are created, expired
objects are removed, and the message list is cleared.
\item \textbf{Sense and communicate} (ordering 2): each agent observes nearby
cells, updates memory, reads incoming messages, and broadcasts its own.
\item \textbf{Think and act} (ordering 3): each agent runs its decision logic
and executes one action (move, pickup, drop, refuel, or wait).
\end{enumerate}

Within ordering~2, agents run \texttt{sense(); communicate();} sequentially in
creation order, each reading the message queue before appending to it. A message
broadcast by agent~$N$ is therefore visible during the same step to agents
$N{+}1$ through~6 (which read after $N$ writes), but not to agents~$1$
through~$N{-}1$ (which already read before $N$ wrote). By ordering~3, all
broadcasts from ordering~2 are in the queue; however, each agent's stored
parsed data reflects only the messages that existed when it called
\texttt{communicate()}, so earlier-ordered agents see later-ordered agents'
broadcasts at the following step rather than the current one. In practice this means fuel station discoveries
and tile or hole sightings propagate to the full team by the following step at
the latest, and to later-ordered agents within the same step.

\subsection{A* Pathfinding}

Navigation is handled by \texttt{AstarPathGenerator}, which computes
obstacle-avoiding paths using the standard A* algorithm with Manhattan distance
as the heuristic. The planner wraps path generation with an affordability check:
before committing to a goal, it verifies that the agent has enough fuel to reach
the target and return to the fuel station. In short-lifetime environments the
planner also rejects targets whose estimated remaining lifetime is shorter than
the steps required to arrive.

\subsection{Memory and Environment Detection}

\texttt{SmartTWAgentMemory} stores observation timestamps for every seen cell,
tile, and hole. These timestamps drive three runtime signals:

\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{isShortLifetime()} --- true when observed object disappearance
rates suggest objects expire in roughly 30 steps or fewer.
\item \texttt{isDense()} --- true when the observed object creation rate is high.
\item \texttt{isLargeGrid()} --- true when the map appears to exceed roughly
60 cells on a side.
\end{itemize}

These signals are estimated from observations rather than hardcoded to a
specific configuration, so the same agents adapt correctly to both Config~1 and
Config~2 without any parameter changes.

\section{Communication Protocol}

Agents communicate via a global broadcast channel. Because ordering~2 runs
agents sequentially, a broadcast from agent~$N$ reaches later-ordered agents
within the same step and earlier-ordered agents by the following step at the
latest; the full team has the message within one step. The base class parses all
incoming messages and stores results in shared protected fields, so specialist
agents consume parsed data directly without writing any parsing code themselves.

Table~\ref{tab:messages} lists the nine message types.

\begin{table*}[t]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}

\textbf{Type} & \textbf{Format} & \textbf{Purpose} \\
\midrule
\texttt{FUEL}     & \texttt{x,y}           & Fuel station location \\
\texttt{TILE}     & \texttt{x,y,time}      & New tile sighting \\
\texttt{HOLE}     & \texttt{x,y,time}      & New hole sighting \\
\texttt{GONE}     & \texttt{x,y}           & Object disappeared \\
\texttt{CLAIM}    & \texttt{x,y}           & Agent is targeting this cell \\
\texttt{LOW}      & \texttt{agentId,fuel}  & Low-fuel warning \\
\texttt{EXPIRING} & \texttt{x,y,reason}    & Target likely to expire \\

Communication improves team performance in four ways. First, \textbf{faster
discovery sharing}: fuel station discovery by any one agent immediately benefits
all five others. Second, \textbf{deduplication}: CLAIM messages prevent two
agents from wasting movement on the same target. Third, \textbf{freshness
control}: EXPIRING warnings let all agents avoid routing toward targets that are
about to vanish. Fourth, \textbf{coverage coordination}: HOTSPOT and SWAP
messages guide exploration toward productive and uncovered regions.

\section{Specialist Agents}

Each specialist inherits all base capabilities and adds a focused decision layer
in \texttt{think()}.

\subsection{FuelScoutAgent}

\texttt{FuelScoutAgent} is designed to accelerate fuel station discovery.
While the station is unknown, it uses a search radius of 20 cells ---
significantly wider than the base agent's lawnmower pattern --- to jump between
zones and cover the map faster. Its refuel threshold adapts to grid size
(\texttt{isLargeGrid() ? 250 : 175}) to ensure it never runs critically low
before the station is found. Once fuel is known and broadcast, it reverts to
standard base behaviour. It also sends \texttt{LOW} warnings when fuel drops
below 150, alerting teammates to shift coverage away from fuel-scarce regions.

\subsection{TileHunterAgent}

\texttt{TileHunterAgent} focuses on efficient tile collection through adaptive
batching and conflict-free claiming. Its primary behavioural parameter is the
tile target: in stable, long-lifetime environments (Config 1) it batches up to
three tiles before delivering, amortising the fuel cost of a delivery trip over
three reward points; in short-lifetime environments (Config 2) it delivers after
every single tile to avoid holes expiring while it is still collecting. This adaptive strategy is necessary, if we perform batching in a volatile environment we risk the agent arriving at
a hole that no longer exists, while delivering one tile at a time in a stable
environment wastes fuel on unnecessary round trips.

To maximise efficiency, when a tile target is identified, \texttt{TileHunterAgent} immediately claims it
in shared memory before moving, ensuring no teammate independently selects the
same target and duplicates the trip. Before committing to any target, it also
applies a reachability check via \texttt{isReachableBeforeExpiry()}, comparing
the Manhattan distance to the target against its remaining lifetime using
environment-adaptive thresholds (0.65 for short-lifetime, 0.85 for
long-lifetime environments). This prevents the agent from chasing tiles or holes
that were observed earlier in memory but are unlikely to still exist on arrival.
The thresholds were tightened from an earlier 0.7/0.9 configuration after
observing that agents were committing to targets near the end of their lifetime
and arriving to find them gone.

When no viable tile is known in memory, the agent does not fall back immediately
to a blind lawnmower sweep. Instead, it first checks for \texttt{HOTSPOT}
broadcasts from the \texttt{DeliveryOptimizerAgent} and navigates toward the
nearest known tile-dense cluster. This biases exploration toward productive
regions of the grid. Only if no hotspot is known does it revert to the base
agent's systematic lawnmower exploration pattern.

\subsection{HoleFillerAgent}

\texttt{HoleFillerAgent} serves a dual role: it is both a delivery agent and a
team-wide warning system against wasted trips. Before committing to any hole, it
runs two sequential validity checks. First, it consults the shared
\texttt{expiringTargets} list populated by teammate \texttt{EXPIRING} broadcasts;
if the candidate hole appears on this list, the plan is voided immediately and
the agent seeks an alternative. Second, it independently projects whether the
hole will still exist on arrival by comparing the Manhattan distance to the
target against the hole's remaining lifetime (current time minus observation
timestamp subtracted from the total configured lifetime). The threshold applied
to this projection adapts to environment volatility: 0.65 in short-lifetime
environments and 0.85 in long-lifetime ones. If the projected travel cost
exceeds the threshold fraction of remaining lifetime, the hole is considered
unreachable and the plan is abandoned.

Critically, when \texttt{HoleFillerAgent} detects an imminent expiry through its
own projection, it does not simply replan silently. It broadcasts an
\texttt{EXPIRING} message to the entire team, ensuring that any other agent
currently committed to the same hole also voids its plan simultaneously. This
propagation prevents scenarios where multiple agents independently arrive at the
same expired location, each having wasted the fuel for the trip.

To maintain team-wide hole awareness beyond individual sensor ranges,
\texttt{HoleFillerAgent} periodically rebroadcasts known hole positions from its
memory. The rebroadcast interval is adaptive: every 10 simulation steps in
short-lifetime environments (Config 2) and every 15 steps in long-lifetime
environments (Config 1). The freshness (age penalty) cutoff applied before rebroadcasting is
equally adaptive, only holes younger than 0.5$\times$ their total lifetime
are rebroadcast in volatile environments, and 0.7$\times$ in stable ones. This
ensures that teammates receive only actionable hole information rather than stale
entries that are unlikely to still exist, reducing noise in the shared message
channel.

\subsection{ExplorerAgent}

\texttt{ExplorerAgent} ensures systematic map coverage. It uses
\texttt{getLeastVisitedTarget()} with a search radius that adapts to grid size
(\texttt{isLargeGrid() ? 20 : 10}) to always move toward the least-recently-seen
area in its assigned zone. It biases movement toward \texttt{HOTSPOT} positions
broadcast by the \texttt{DeliveryOptimizerAgent}, but only when the hotspot lies
within its own zone. When coverage exceeds 80\% it broadcasts a \texttt{SWAP}
message to signal zone completion, allowing the team to shift exploration
capacity elsewhere.

\subsection{DeliveryOptimizerAgent}

\texttt{DeliveryOptimizerAgent} maximises reward per fuel unit by replacing
simple nearest-hole routing with a composite scoring function and structured
decision cascade. Its \texttt{think()} method implements seven sequential gates,
from fuel emergency handling through tile hunting, en-route batching, delivery,
and hotspot navigation, with each gate only activating if higher-priority
conditions are unmet.

When selecting a delivery target, the agent scores all known unclaimed holes
using the following function:

\begin{equation}
\text{score}(h) = d(a, h) - w_b \cdot \text{bonus}(h) + w_f \cdot
\frac{\text{age}(h)}{\text{lifetime}}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $d(a, h)$ is the Manhattan distance from the agent to hole $h$,
$\text{bonus}(h)$ is a tile proximity bonus computed as the sum of
$1/(1 + d(h, t))$ over all tiles $t$ within a cluster radius of $h$, and
$\text{age}(h)/\text{lifetime}$ is the fractional age of the hole penalising
stale targets. The weights $w_b$ (bonus weight) and cluster radius adapt to
environment density: 4.5 and 8 cells in dense environments, 1.5 and 12 cells in
sparse ones. The freshness penalty weight $w_f$ was reduced from 15.0 to 10.0
after observing that the original value over-penalised slightly older but
perfectly reachable nearby holes, pushing the agent toward distant fresh targets
unnecessarily. The dual-threshold expiry projection already filters truly
unreachable holes, so the freshness penalty serves only as a soft preference
rather than a hard filter.

Before scoring, each candidate hole is validated against \texttt{EXPIRING}
broadcasts, arrival-time projection (thresholds 0.65/0.85), and fuel round-trip
affordability. Holes failing any check are excluded from scoring entirely.

When carrying tiles but below the delivery threshold, the agent performs en-route
batching: it identifies tiles whose collection adds at most a configurable detour
budget beyond the direct path to the target hole. The budget is 2 steps in
short-lifetime environments and 4 steps in long-lifetime ones, ensuring the
agent never risks a hole expiring by taking too large a detour.

To prevent goal oscillation, where an agent switches targets every step as
scores fluctuate, the agent commits to a selected hole for a number of steps
proportional to the distance to that target, bounded between a minimum of 6 and
a maximum of 18 steps. The commitment is released early only if an
\texttt{EXPIRING} signal is received for the committed target or fuel becomes
critical.

Moreover, \texttt{DeliveryOptimizerAgent} acts as the team's dedicated
\texttt{HOTSPOT} broadcaster. It groups all remembered tiles into $7 \times 7$
spatial clusters, computes a centroid and density for each cluster containing at
least two tiles, and broadcasts the two densest clusters as \texttt{HOTSPOT}
messages. To reduce channel noise, broadcasts are throttled to once every five
simulation steps.

\subsection{SmarterReplanningAgent}


\texttt{SmarterReplanningAgent} acts as the system's reliability expert by implementing the adaptive cautious agent concept from \shortcite{pollack1990}, applying predictive plan validation before any movement is executed. At each step, before acting on its current plan, it runs three sequential checks on the active goal.

The first check consults the shared \texttt{expiringTargets} list. If any
teammate has already broadcast an \texttt{EXPIRING} signal for the current goal,
the plan is aborted immediately without further computation, since the target has
already been identified as unreachable by another agent, avoiding redundancy.

The second check is an independent arrival-time projection. The agent computes
the Manhattan distance to the goal and compares it against the goal's estimated
remaining lifetime derived from its observation timestamp. The expiry threshold
adapts to environment volatility: 0.65 in short-lifetime environments (Config 2)
and 0.85 in long-lifetime ones (Config 1). These thresholds were tightened from
an original 0.7/0.9 configuration to trigger earlier plan abandonment, which
proved especially important in Config 2 where targets expire within 30 steps. If
the check fails, the agent broadcasts an \texttt{EXPIRING} warning before
replanning, propagating the detection to teammates who may be pursuing the same
goal.

The third check verifies fuel round-trip affordability. The agent estimates the
total fuel cost of reaching the goal and returning to the known fuel station,
adding a safety margin that scales with grid size (\texttt{max(50, gridWidth /
4)}). If current fuel falls below this estimate, the plan is voided and the
agent navigates directly to the fuel station, prioritising survival over the
current delivery. This check prevents the failure mode observed in earlier
versions, where agents would commit to a distant goal, run out of fuel mid-path,
and contribute nothing for the remainder of the simulation.

The agent then executes its planned move only if all three checks pass. This
conservative posture trades some aggressiveness, the agent will abandon goals
that a less cautious agent might successfully complete for reliability,
virtually eliminating fuel deaths from over-commitment and reducing the number
of wasted trips the team collectively makes to expired targets.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Phase Development (Config~1)}

Table~\ref{tab:phases} shows the average reward achieved at each development

Phase~1 established a survival baseline: \texttt{SmartTWAgent} and
\texttt{SmartTWAgentMemory} replaced the default agent with fuel-aware movement,
memory decay, and opportunistic tile pickup. The system reacted to local events
but could not yet route toward high-value work.

Phase~2 added \texttt{SmartTWPlanner} with A* pathfinding. Agents stopped
wandering and began executing goal-directed plans. The first major reward jump
followed because the same map knowledge was now exploited efficiently.

Phase~3 introduced multi-agent coordination: zone-based exploration,
message broadcasts, shared tile and hole knowledge, and claim-based
deconfliction. The score jump from Phase~2 to Phase~3 (160.3 to 541.5, a 3.4$\times$
increase) was the largest in the project and came from structural rather than
incremental improvement: six agents began working as a team rather than as
independent individuals.

Phase~4 unfolded in three steps. First, adaptive improvements to
\texttt{SmartTWAgent} and \texttt{SmartTWAgentMemory} directly: runtime
environment detection, adaptive fuel safety margins, and smarter planning
heuristics. Second, a two-stage architectural transition: the uniform agent loop was first
broken into individually instantiated person-named subclasses (one per team
member), then renamed to role-named classes (\texttt{TileHunterAgent},
\texttt{HoleFillerAgent}, etc.), both stages initially delegating entirely to
the shared base. Third, follow-up commits
added the substantive role-specific logic --- tighter expiry thresholds,
freshness-aware delivery scoring, reachability checks, and throttled broadcasts.
The 864.3 average reflects this full three-step progression; the largest gains
came from the adaptive base improvements and the coordination already established
in Phase~3, with role-specific behaviour providing targeted quality refinements
on top.

\section{Analysis}

\subsection{Strengths}

\textbf{Shared-base scalability.} Implementing pathfinding, memory, and
communication once in the base class means every algorithmic improvement
benefits all six agents simultaneously. This was the most important engineering
decision in the project.

\textbf{Coordination under partial observability.} Without communication, each
agent is limited to what it can directly observe. The broadcast protocol
converts six local observers into a distributed shared-intelligence system.
Claims prevent duplicated effort; EXPIRING warnings stop wasted movement;
HOTSPOT messages direct exploration toward productive regions.

\textbf{Measured performance trajectory.} The phase scores confirm that each
architectural addition produced a measurable gain. The largest gain (Phase~3
coordination) accounts for the majority of total improvement, validating the
design priority of getting coordination right before tuning specialisation.

\textbf{Cross-agent idea borrowing.} Several mechanisms were refined across
agents. For example, \texttt{DeliveryOptimizerAgent} explicitly combines expiry
projection (originally developed for \texttt{SmarterReplanningAgent}), hotspot
routing, and freshness-aware scoring into a single delivery policy. Good ideas
spread through the team incrementally.

\subsection{Weaknesses}

\textbf{Config~2 structural risk.} The 10-run validation produced a positive
average reward (888.6), but the environment's structure creates latent reliability
risk not fully captured by the aggregate. The 80$\times$80 grid creates a larger
search space for the fuel station; delayed discovery forces the whole team into
conservative routing. Short object lifetimes increase the chance that remembered
targets expire before an agent arrives, even with predictive expiry checking. Per-trial variance (individual min/max across the 10 trials) was not separately
recorded for Config~2, so the spread of outcomes is unquantified.

\textbf{Early-run lifetime detection.} The runtime environment classifier
requires enough observed disappearances to estimate object lifetime accurately.
In the first hundred steps of a Config~2 run, agents may still believe they
are in a stable environment and apply overly generous planning horizons.

\textbf{Fuel station discovery bottleneck.} Many decisions across the system
depend on the fuel station being found early. On a large map with an unlucky
starting layout, delayed discovery creates cascading conservative behaviour
across all agents until the station is located.

\subsection{Coordination Tradeoff}

The fundamental tradeoff in any multi-agent system is between the parallelism
benefit --- six agents covering the map simultaneously, collecting tiles, and
filling holes in parallel --- and the coordination cost of communication,
claim management, and zone assignment overhead. This system is strongest when
the environment gives coordination time to pay off: Config~1, with its stable,
sparse objects, rewards careful planning and reliable delivery. Config~2 tests
the limits because the environment changes fast enough that shared information
can become stale before the team acts on it.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented a six-agent TileWorld multi-agent system that improves from a
survival baseline (Phase~1, avg.\ 32.1) to a coordinated specialist team
(Phase~4, avg.\ 864.3 on Config~1) through four development phases.
A 10-run Phase~4 validation on Config~2 achieved an average reward of 888.6;
the four-phase development history is documented for Config~1 only. The key design insight is the two-layer separation between shared
infrastructure and role specialisation: improvements to the base layer compound
across all agents, while specialist layers add targeted decision quality without
duplicating infrastructure.

The most impactful single change was the introduction of multi-agent
coordination in Phase~3, confirming the importance of communication and
deconfliction over individual agent optimisation. Phase~4 role specialisation
added further gains by covering different failure modes of the task: fuel
discovery, tile collection efficiency, delivery reliability, map coverage,
high-value routing, and predictive failure prevention.

Future work could improve Config~2 reliability through two main directions:
earlier fuel station discovery via a dedicated fast-scout strategy that
temporarily overrides zone assignments at the start of a run, and adaptive
lifetime thresholds that update more aggressively in the first 50 steps based
on early disappearance observations.

Through our this project Gemini 3.1 and Claude 4.6 Sonnet were used to correct grammatical and phrasing errors, and format the citations. Claude 4.6 Sonnet was also used to help with coding related tasks, especially with Java . Other than this, no AI system was used during the preparation of this report, and it mirrors the group's understanding of the concepts presented.
